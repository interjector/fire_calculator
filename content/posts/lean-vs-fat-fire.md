## Lean vs. Fat FIRE: Finding Your Number

One of the most personal decisions in any FIRE journey is choosing your target lifestyle — and the number that funds it. The spectrum runs from **Lean FIRE** (minimalist, frugal, fastest to reach) to **Fat FIRE** (comfortable, lifestyle-maintaining, maximum optionality). Neither is objectively better. They're different answers to the same question:

> *What does financial freedom look like for you?*

Getting that answer right matters more than any spreadsheet optimization.

---

## Lean FIRE: Freedom Through Frugality

**Target spending:** $25,000–$40,000/year
**Portfolio needed:** ~$625,000–$1,000,000 (at 4% withdrawal)
**Timeline:** Often 8–15 years from a median income with a high savings rate

Lean FIRE is built on a simple bet: you can live a rich, meaningful life on significantly less than the American average, and the freedom you gain from reaching FI early outweighs the lifestyle constraints.

Lean FIRE adherents tend to live in low cost-of-living areas — smaller cities, rural areas, or abroad — own their home outright (often a small or unconventional one), drive used cars or go car-free, cook at home, and find fulfillment in experiences, community, and projects rather than consumption.

### Advantages

- **Achievable fastest.** A 40% savings rate from a $75K income can get you to $800K in under 15 years.
- **Less market exposure.** A $750K portfolio is more achievable and easier to manage than $2.5M.
- **Forced intentionality.** Living lean teaches you which spending actually makes you happy — and most people find the answer is "less than I thought."

### Trade-offs

- **Thin margins.** Less cushion for healthcare costs, major home repairs, aging parents, or an unexpected shift in circumstances.
- **Vulnerable to lifestyle drift.** If your actual spending creeps up post-retirement, the math unravels faster.
- **Geographic constraints.** Lean FIRE often requires choosing where you live based on cost, which may conflict with family, community, or preferences.

---

## Fat FIRE: Freedom Without Compromise

**Target spending:** $100,000+/year
**Portfolio needed:** $2,500,000+ (at 4% withdrawal)
**Timeline:** Often 20–35 years, depending on income and savings rate

Fat FIRE takes a different bet: you've worked hard, you like your lifestyle, and you don't want to significantly constrain yourself in retirement. The goal isn't to spend less — it's to accumulate enough that you can spend what you want indefinitely.

Fat FIRE adherents often have higher incomes (making the larger number more achievable), want flexibility for travel, dining, entertainment, and family generosity, and prioritize the psychological comfort of a large financial cushion.

### Advantages

- **Maximum flexibility.** Healthcare crises, helping adult children, unexpected expenses — a larger portfolio absorbs shocks that would derail a leaner plan.
- **No lifestyle adjustment.** You're not moving, cutting subscriptions, or counting restaurant meals. Life continues.
- **Room to be generous.** Fat FIRE portfolios often generate enough that you can give meaningfully — to family, charity, or causes you care about — without threatening your retirement security.

### Trade-offs

- **Longer accumulation phase.** More years of work means delayed freedom. For some, this is a significant cost.
- **High savings rate required.** To reach $2.5M+, you need to save a substantial portion of a high income — which can feel restrictive during the accumulation years.
- **Relative deprivation.** Working toward Fat FIRE often means watching colleagues spend more freely now while you save aggressively for later.

## Regular FIRE: The Pragmatic Middle

**Target spending:** $50,000–$80,000/year
**Portfolio needed:** $1,250,000–$2,000,000

The most common FIRE target. Regular FIRE covers the full range of American middle-class lifestyles — especially with paid-off housing reducing expenses — without requiring either extreme frugality or an outsized income.

Regular FIRE is attainable on above-average-but-not-extraordinary incomes with a disciplined savings rate. It's the "default" FIRE that most calculator examples use.

---

## Barista FIRE: The Hybrid Approach

**Structure:** Partial retirement + part-time work
**Portfolio:** Smaller than full FI (enough that PT income covers the gap)

Barista FIRE is named after the idea of working a low-stress job with benefits — like a barista — while your portfolio does the heavy lifting. You leave your primary career (and its income, stress, and time requirements), take on something easier and more enjoyable part-time, and let your investments grow toward full FI.

The financial logic: even modest part-time income ($15–25K/year) dramatically changes the math. If your portfolio is at $500K and your annual spending is $55K, you'd normally need another $900K to hit full FI. But if you're earning $20K from part-time work, you only need $35K from your portfolio — which $500K can sustain indefinitely at 7% withdrawal. You're effectively already FI with a small side income.

**The real appeal of Barista FIRE isn't just financial.** It's the transition. Going from full-time career directly to full retirement is jarring for many people. Barista FIRE creates an intermediate state — you're no longer trapped by a career, but you're still engaged, earning, and potentially covered for healthcare through an employer.

---

## Coast FIRE: The Portfolio Milestone

**Structure:** Stop contributing; let compound growth carry you to FI
**Target:** The invested amount that will grow to your FI number by traditional retirement age

Coast FIRE answers a specific question: *At what point can I stop contributing to my retirement accounts and still be okay?*

The math is driven by compound interest. If you have $300,000 invested at age 35 and assume 7% real annual returns, that $300K will grow to approximately $1.6M by age 65 without another dollar of contribution. If your FI number is $1.5M, you've "coasted."

Once you hit your Coast number, you can:
- Reduce work hours or income significantly (you only need to cover current expenses, not save)
- Take lower-paying but more fulfilling work
- Take extended breaks or sabbaticals
- Shift career entirely without worrying about retirement savings

Coast FIRE is an underrated milestone. Many people are already there without realizing it.

## How to Choose Your Target

There's no formula for deciding which FIRE type fits you. A few questions that can help:

**1. What does your ideal day look like?**
Design the life first, then calculate the number. Are you living simply and spending time outdoors? Working part-time on creative projects? Hosting dinner parties in a well-appointed home? Traveling business class? Each of these implies a different spending level.

**2. What does money actually buy you?**
Research consistently shows that beyond a certain income threshold (often estimated around $75–100K for most Americans), additional consumption produces diminishing returns on life satisfaction. If you're already content with a moderate lifestyle, the extra years of work to reach Fat FIRE may cost more than they gain.

**3. What's your risk tolerance?**
A Lean FIRE portfolio has less margin for error. A sequence-of-returns failure early in a $700K retirement can force you back to work. A $2.5M portfolio can absorb a decade of poor returns and still survive. How much uncertainty can you live with?

**4. Can you use Barista or Coast as milestones?**
You don't have to go from full-time career to full retirement. Consider mapping out the milestones: Coast FIRE → Barista FIRE → Regular FIRE. Each milestone is meaningful and changes your options in the present, not just at some far-off endpoint.

**5. How much flexibility do you have?**
Some expenses are fixed (housing, healthcare, basic food). Others are discretionary. If the bulk of your spending is discretionary, you have natural flexibility to cut in bad market years — which means you can safely aim for a leaner number. If your spending is dominated by fixed costs, lean harder toward a larger portfolio.

## Comparing All Five Types

| FIRE Type | Annual Spending | Portfolio Needed | Key Trade-off |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Lean | $25–40K | $625K–$1M | Fastest, but least cushion |
| Barista | Variable | ~50–75% of FI | Needs part-time income |
| Regular | $50–80K | $1.25M–$2M | Most common, balanced |
| Fat | $100K+ | $2.5M+ | Maximum freedom, longest accumulation |
| Coast | N/A | Depends on age | Milestone, not an endpoint |
