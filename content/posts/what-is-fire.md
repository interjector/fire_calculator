## What is FIRE?

FIRE stands for **Financial Independence, Retire Early**. At its core, the idea is simple: accumulate enough invested assets that your investment returns can cover your living expenses indefinitely — giving you the freedom to choose how you spend your time, without depending on a paycheck.

The word "retire" can be misleading. FIRE isn't about quitting work and watching TV for the rest of your life. It's about reaching a point where work becomes *optional*. Many people who achieve FIRE continue working — they just do it on their own terms. They take on projects they care about, start businesses, travel, raise children, or contribute to causes. The difference is they're never trapped.

---

## A Brief History

The intellectual roots of FIRE trace back to the 1992 book *Your Money or Your Life* by Vicki Robin and Joe Dominguez. The book reframed money not as a goal in itself, but as "life energy" — the hours of your finite life you trade for it. That reframe is still the philosophical backbone of the movement.

FIRE didn't reach mainstream consciousness until 2011, when software engineer Pete Adeney — known online as **Mr. Money Mustache** — began blogging about retiring at 30 by saving aggressively and living on a fraction of a typical American income. His core insight: most people spend far more than they need to, and with a high savings rate, financial independence is achievable far earlier than society suggests.

Since then, the FIRE community has grown into a global movement with Reddit communities, podcasts, books, and conferences — with paths ranging from extreme frugality to comfortable lifestyle maintenance.

## The FI Number

The central concept in FIRE is your **FI Number** — the portfolio size at which you're financially independent:

> **FI Number = Annual Spending ÷ Safe Withdrawal Rate**

At the commonly used 4% withdrawal rate, this simplifies to **25× your annual spending**.

| Annual Spending | FI Number (4% Rule) |
|----------------|---------------------|
| $30,000 | $750,000 |
| $50,000 | $1,250,000 |
| $75,000 | $1,875,000 |
| $100,000 | $2,500,000 |

Once your invested assets hit this number, a diversified portfolio has historically been able to sustain your withdrawals for decades — through market crashes, high inflation, and everything else. Your money earns more each year than you spend, so your portfolio replenishes itself.

## The Three Phases

Most FIRE journeys move through three phases:

**1. Accumulation**

You're earning more than you spend, and investing the difference aggressively. This is the wealth-building phase. Early on, your contributions matter most. Later, compound returns take over — your portfolio grows faster than you can contribute to it. The key drivers are your savings rate and time in market.

**2. The Transition**

Your portfolio generates meaningful passive income, but you might not be fully FI yet. Many people use this phase intentionally — shifting to work they find more fulfilling, reducing hours, or exploring Barista FIRE or Coast FIRE (more on those below). The transition phase is often the most interesting part of the journey.

**3. Financial Independence**

Your investment income covers your expenses. Work is optional. You can retire fully, keep working because you want to, travel, build something, or simply have the security of knowing you never *have* to accept a situation you don't want. That optionality is what FIRE is really about.

## What FIRE Isn't

A few misconceptions worth addressing:

**It's not just for high earners.** Savings rate matters more than income. Someone earning $60K and saving 40% will reach FI faster than someone earning $150K and saving 10%.

**It's not deprivation.** FIRE is about intentionally aligning your spending with your values, not cutting everything you enjoy. Many FIRE adherents live rich, full lives — they've just eliminated spending that doesn't actually make them happier.

**It's not extreme frugality by default.** The spectrum runs from Lean FIRE (minimalist, ~$1M target) to Fat FIRE ($100K+/year, $2.5M+ target). You choose the lifestyle you want, then build toward the number that funds it.

---

## FIRE Types at a Glance

The FIRE movement has split into distinct paths depending on your target lifestyle:

**Lean FIRE** — Live on $25–40K/year. Target portfolio ~$625K–$1M. Possible with frugal habits, especially in low cost-of-living areas. The fastest path to FI, with the smallest margin for error in retirement.

**Barista FIRE** — Partially retire. Work part-time at something low-stress (enough for income and possibly healthcare benefits) while your portfolio continues to grow. You're not fully FI yet, but you've escaped the career grind.

**Regular FIRE** — The most common target. $50–80K/year, ~$1.25M–$2M needed. Covers most American lifestyles comfortably, especially with paid-off housing.

**Fat FIRE** — Maintain or upgrade your current lifestyle in retirement. $100K+/year, $2.5M+ target. More cushion, longer timeline, maximum flexibility.

**Coast FIRE** — You've invested enough that compound growth alone will carry you to full FI by traditional retirement age. You stop contributing, shift to lower-pressure work, and "coast." Once you hit your Coast number, every dollar you earn is yours to spend.

## Getting Started

The path to FIRE begins with two numbers: **what you spend** and **what you save**. Track your actual spending for a month or two, then calculate your savings rate (savings ÷ gross income). From there, you can estimate your FI timeline.

The FIRE Calculator on this site lets you model your specific situation — input your current portfolio, contribution rate, and target spending, and it will project your FIRE age, show how different scenarios play out, and stress-test your plan with Monte Carlo simulation.

The journey is longer for some than others, but the direction is the same: spend less than you earn, invest the difference, and build toward a life you don't need to escape from.
