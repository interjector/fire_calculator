## The 4% Rule Explained

If you've spent any time in the FIRE community, you've encountered the 4% rule. It's the most widely cited guideline in retirement planning, and the foundation for virtually every FIRE calculation. Understanding where it came from, how it works, and — critically — where it breaks down is essential for building a realistic plan.

---

## Origins: The Trinity Study

In 1998, three professors at Trinity University — Phillip Cooley, Carl Hubbard, and Daniel Walz — published a paper titled *Retirement Savings: Choosing a Withdrawal Rate That Is Sustainable.* This became known as the **Trinity Study**.

The researchers analyzed historical stock and bond market data across every rolling 30-year period from 1926 to 1995. They tested what happened to a portfolio under different asset allocations (stocks/bonds) and withdrawal rates, looking for the rate at which the portfolio survived — meaning it didn't hit zero.

Their finding: **a portfolio invested 50–75% in stocks with a 4% initial withdrawal rate, adjusted annually for inflation, survived 95–100% of all 30-year historical periods tested.**

In many of those scenarios, the portfolio didn't just survive — it grew substantially, leaving a large inheritance.

---

## How It Works

The rule itself is straightforward:

1. In your first year of retirement, withdraw **4% of your starting portfolio**.
2. Each subsequent year, **adjust that withdrawal for inflation** (not the portfolio's performance).
3. Your portfolio's investment returns (historically ~7% real) replenish what you've withdrawn and then some in most years.

**Example:**

You retire with $1,000,000.

| Year | Inflation | Withdrawal |
|------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | — | $40,000 (4%) |
| 2 | 3% | $41,200 |
| 3 | 3% | $42,436 |
| 10 | cumulative ~30% | ~$52,000 |
| 20 | cumulative ~80% | ~$72,000 |

Meanwhile, your portfolio continues to grow in most market environments, more than offsetting the withdrawals over time.

---

## The Math Behind It

A well-diversified equity portfolio has historically returned approximately **7% annually in real (inflation-adjusted) terms** over long periods. At a 4% withdrawal rate, your portfolio should be growing faster than you're drawing it down — in an average year.

The danger isn't average years. It's **sequence of returns risk**.

If the market crashes hard in your first few retirement years, you're forced to sell shares at depressed prices to fund your living expenses. Selling low permanently impairs your portfolio's recovery — you have fewer shares to benefit when prices eventually rebound. This is the mechanism that causes retirement plans to fail.

The Trinity Study tested across all historical sequences, including periods that began right before the Great Depression and the oil crises of the 1970s. The 4% rule's success rate reflects its resilience even in those worst-case sequences.

## Where the 4% Rule Breaks Down

### Problem 1: 30-Year Assumption

The Trinity Study was designed for people retiring around age 65 — a 30-year horizon. If you retire at 40, you need your portfolio to last 50–60 years. The further out you project, the more uncertainty compounds.

Research by financial planner Michael Kitces and others suggests the success rate for 4% withdrawals begins to fall below 90% at 40+ year horizons when using historical US data.

### Problem 2: US-Centric Data

The original study used US stock and bond markets. The US had an unusually favorable 20th century. Studies using global equity data suggest slightly lower safe withdrawal rates — closer to 3.5% for international markets.

### Problem 3: High Valuations

Some researchers argue that current high stock market valuations (measured by CAPE ratio) suggest future returns will be lower than the historical average, meaning the 4% rule may be overly optimistic for people retiring today.

---

## Adjusted Rates for Early Retirees

Based on extended research and analysis, here are more conservative guidelines by retirement age:

| Retirement Age | Horizon | Suggested Rate |
|---------------|---------|----------------|
| 65+ | ~30 years | 4.0% |
| 55–64 | ~35–40 years | 3.5% |
| 45–54 | ~40–50 years | 3.25% |
| Under 45 | 50+ years | 3.0–3.25% |

These aren't guarantees — they're planning benchmarks based on historical analysis. The further out you retire, the more conservative you should be in your baseline assumptions.

## The Guardrails Strategy

A more dynamic approach gaining popularity is the **guardrails method**, originally developed by financial planner Jonathan Guyton:

- **Upper rail:** If your portfolio has grown enough that your current withdrawal represents less than 3.2% of its value, you can increase your spending by 10%.
- **Lower rail:** If your portfolio has fallen enough that your current withdrawal represents more than 4.8% of its value, you reduce your spending by 10%.

This approach dramatically increases success rates across all time horizons by allowing you to respond to actual market conditions rather than mechanically withdrawing a fixed amount regardless of what the market is doing.

A simpler version many FIRE practitioners use: *be willing to cut discretionary spending by 15–20% in the first 3–5 years of a major bear market.*

---

## Beyond the 4% Rule

The 4% rule is a starting point, not a complete retirement income strategy. A few supplementary considerations:

**Social Security.** If you're early-retiring at 45 but will collect Social Security starting at 67, you only need to fund 22 years from your portfolio before SS kicks in. That meaningfully changes your math.

**Part-time income.** Even modest income ($10–20K/year) significantly reduces portfolio draw-down risk early in retirement, when sequence-of-returns risk is highest.

**Spending flexibility.** The 4% rule assumes fixed inflation-adjusted withdrawals. In practice, most retirees spend less as they age (the "spending smile" phenomenon). If you can flex spending in bad market years, your plan is far more robust.

**Home equity.** A paid-off home is a significant asset and reduces housing expense risk. Many FIRE planners treat it as insurance against worst-case scenarios.

## Practical Application

The FIRE Calculator on this site uses your specified withdrawal rate (default 4%) to calculate your target portfolio:

> Target Portfolio = Annual Spending ÷ Withdrawal Rate

It also runs Monte Carlo simulation to stress-test your plan across thousands of randomized return sequences — which is more rigorous than the point-in-time historical analysis of the Trinity Study.

The bottom line: use the 4% rule as your planning benchmark, adjust downward if you're retiring very early, build in spending flexibility, and plan for multiple income sources. It's a powerful tool — just not an unconditional guarantee.
